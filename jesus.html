Special Analysis

Why the "Christian" Party Scores Last

The Constitution Party explicitly grounds its platform in the Bible and claims to represent Christian governance.
Yet it scores lower against the actual teachings of Jesus than any other party. Here's why — with receipts.

Origin · 01Founded by a Theocrat, Not a Theologian

The Constitution Party was founded in 1992 by Howard Phillips and is directly rooted in the theology of R.J. Rushdoony, the father of Christian Reconstructionism, who helped write the party's original 1992 platform. Rushdoony's vision was to replace American law with Old Testament Mosaic law — including the death penalty for blasphemy, adultery, homosexuality, and apostasy.

This is not the Sermon on the Mount. This is Leviticus filtered through 20th-century American nationalism. Jesus never cited Levitical law as the governing framework for society — he consistently superseded it with mercy ("You have heard it said… but I say to you").

Sources: Wikipedia — Constitution Party; Wikipedia — R.J. Rushdoony; Religion Dispatches — "How a Fringe Theocratic Movement Shaped the Religious Right"; Political Research Associates — Christian Reconstructionism

Theology · 02They Follow the Old Testament. Jesus Came to Fulfill and Transform It.

The animating theology behind the Constitution Party — Dominionism and Christian Reconstructionism — is obsessively focused on Old Testament law as a political blueprint. Rushdoony's foundational text was literally called Institutes of Biblical Law, consciously modeled on Calvin, not on the Gospels.

But Jesus's sharpest conflicts with the religious establishment of his day were precisely about over-literal application of religious law at the expense of mercy. He healed on the Sabbath. He forgave the woman the law said to stone. He touched lepers the law said to avoid. The Constitution Party's theological foundation — rule by religious law enforced by state power — is the Pharisee position, not the Jesus position.

Sources: Gospel Coalition — "Christian Reconstruction: R.J. Rushdoony"; ARDA — Christian Reconstructionism Timeline; Surviving Church — "Reconstructionism"

Economics · 03They Oppose Government Care for the Poor — By Design

The party's official platform states explicitly that "charitable giving is most effective when conducted by private parties" and that government has no constitutional authority to be involved in charity or social welfare. They oppose Social Security, national healthcare, and federal aid of any kind.

This is framed as a constitutional argument, but it conveniently aligns with eliminating every structural mechanism for caring for the poor at scale. Jesus's command in Matthew 25 — "whatever you did for the least of these" — was not a suggestion for private individuals while the state looks away. The early Jerusalem church held all things in common (Acts 2:44-45). The Constitution Party's economics would have made that illegal as a violation of property rights.

Sources: Constitution Party National Platform 2024–2028 (constitutionparty.com); American Constitution Party of Colorado platform via Ballotpedia

Nationalism · 04"Christian Nation" Is Exactly What Jesus Rejected

The core Constitution Party identity is that America is — and must be governed as — a Christian nation. Their platform is grounded in "the Bible" as a governing document alongside the Constitution. Their 2024 presidential nominee was Randall Terry, an anti-abortion activist whose political theology explicitly merges church authority and state power.

Jesus was offered exactly this — nationalist messianic kingship — and refused it. The Zealots wanted a warrior-king to restore Israel as a Jewish nation-state. Jesus fled when the crowd tried to make him king by force (John 6:15). "My kingdom is not of this world" (John 18:36) is not a minor footnote — it is a direct rejection of the fusion of religious identity with political-national power. The Constitution Party is, structurally, the Zealot party.

Sources: Constitution Party platform (constitutionparty.com); Wikipedia — Constitution Party; John 6:15, John 18:36

Immigration · 05Nativism vs. "I Was a Stranger and You Welcomed Me"

The party platform affirms "the integrity of the borders of the United States" and supports strict limits on immigration and official English-only governance. Multiple prominent Republicans who joined the Constitution Party did so specifically over immigration restriction — including the founder of the Minuteman Project border-patrol vigilante movement.

Matthew 25:35 is unambiguous: "I was a stranger and you welcomed me." Jesus lists welcoming the stranger alongside feeding the hungry and clothing the naked as the literal criteria for judgment. The Constitution Party's immigration position is a direct contradiction of one of Jesus's most explicit moral tests.

Sources: Constitution Party platform; Wikipedia — Constitution Party (Jim Gilchrist / Minuteman Project); Matthew 25:35

Power · 06They Seek Dominion. Jesus Washed Feet.

The theological DNA of the party — Dominionism — is named for its central goal: Christians taking "dominion" over all spheres of society including government, education, economics, and law. Rushdoony's followers called for "a multi-generational transformation" that would eventually bring all of human society under Christian governance.

Jesus's model of leadership was servant leadership taken to its most radical expression — the teacher washing his students' feet (John 13). He explicitly contrasted this with how worldly rulers operate: "The rulers of the Gentiles lord it over them... but it shall not be so among you" (Matthew 20:25-26). The drive for dominion over all social institutions is precisely the mode of power Jesus condemned. The name of the movement is, ironically, a confession of the problem.

Sources: ARDA — Christian Reconstructionism Timeline; Political Research Associates — "Christian Reconstructionism"; Gospel Coalition review of McVicar; Matthew 20:25-26, John 13:1-17

The Verdict: Sincere but Deeply Mistaken

This is not an argument that Constitution Party members are bad people or insincere in their faith. Many are deeply committed Christians acting on genuine conviction. The critique is theological and structural, not personal.

The problem is that the party's actual governing theology — Christian Reconstructionism, Dominionism, Old Testament theonomy — is sourced almost entirely from the Pentateuch and filtered through 20th-century American conservatism, not from the Gospels. When you read the Sermon on the Mount, the parables, the healing narratives, and the passion story alongside the Constitution Party platform, they are not describing the same moral universe.

Jesus reserved his harshest condemnation for exactly this configuration: powerful religious institutions claiming divine authority, merging religious identity with political power, performing piety publicly while the poor suffer at the gate. Matthew 23 — the "woe to you, scribes and Pharisees" chapter — reads like a diagnostic of Christian nationalism two thousand years before the term existed.

The party that most loudly claims Jesus scores lowest because it has substituted religious power for his actual teachings, which were consistently about subverting religious power on behalf of the excluded, the poor, and the stranger.

Overall Verdict

Democratic Socialists (DSA) and the Green Party score closest — not because Jesus was a policy platform writer, but because their structural commitments to redistribution, anti-militarism, restorative justice, and radical inclusion most faithfully mirror what he actually did and taught.

The tradition of King, Debs, Sanders, and Mamdani is probably the nearest modern analog: a moral critique of wealth concentration as structural violence, combined with anti-imperialism, care for the outsider, and justice over punishment. King's "triple evils" framework — racism, militarism, economic exploitation — maps almost perfectly onto Jesus's own targets.

On abortion specifically: Jesus never mentioned it — not once in any Gospel. Row 11 scores each party not on their legal position but on whether they address the conditions Jesus spent his entire ministry fighting: poverty, lack of healthcare, abandonment of the vulnerable, and a society that leaves people without support. Criminalizing abortion while opposing every program that reduces its root causes is not a position Jesus endorsed. He never spoke to the legal question. He spoke constantly to the human suffering underneath it.

But the most honest answer remains: Jesus would belong to no party and would be a radical dissident to all of them — claimed by everyone, comfortable with no one, and most dangerous to whichever institution was currently wielding religious language to protect its own power.
